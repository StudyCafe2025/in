Formation of Energy Bands

In solids, energy bands form when atoms interact closely, causing their electron energy levels to overlap and combine into continuous ranges of allowed energy states, called bands.

  • Atomic Interactions: Valence electron orbitals overlap when atoms approach.
  • Energy Level Splitting: Creates very closely spaced bands.
  • Valence Band: Band with highest occupied electron energy levels.
  • Conduction Band: Higher energy band that allows electron flow.
  • Band Gap: Energy difference between valence and conduction bands.

These bands determine whether a material behaves as a conductor, semiconductor, or insulator.

Classification Based on Band Theory

Materials are categorized based on their band gap size.

  • Conductors: Valence and conduction bands overlap; high conductivity.
  • Semiconductors: Small band gap; moderate conductivity.
  • Insulators: Large band gap; poor conductivity.

Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic

  • Pure material, no impurities
  • Si and Ge are common examples
  • Equal number of electrons and holes

Extrinsic

  • Doped semiconductors
  • N-type: excess electrons
  • P-type: excess holes

Doping improves conductivity, enabling real-world applications like transistors.

Drift and Diffusion Currents

Drift Current

  • Movement due to electric field
  • Direction follows the field

Diffusion Current

  • Movement due to concentration gradient
  • Occurs without electric field

These are essential for charge transport in diodes and transistors.

Einstein's Equation

E = mc2
  • E: Energy
  • m: Mass
  • c: Speed of light

This equation underpins nuclear physics and mass-energy conversion.

Applications

  • Binding energy in nuclei
  • Nuclear power and bombs
  • Astrophysics (fusion in stars)
