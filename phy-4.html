Introduction to Lasers

The development of laser technology began with Albert Einstein's theoretical work in 1917, where he introduced the concept of stimulated emission. This concept became the foundation for laser action. The first actual laser device was successfully demonstrated in 1960 by Theodore Maiman using a ruby crystal.

Definition

LASER is an acronym for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. It is a device that emits a highly focused, monochromatic, and coherent beam of light through the process of optical amplification.

How Lasers Work

In a laser, atoms or molecules in the active medium are excited to higher energy levels. When these particles return to a lower energy state, they emit photons. In the case of stimulated emission, one photon can trigger other excited particles to emit more photons of the same energy, phase, and direction, creating a coherent light beam.

Key Features of Laser Light

  • High Coherence: All emitted photons are in phase.
  • Monochromatic: Laser emits light of a single wavelength or frequency.
  • High Intensity: Laser beams have high energy concentration within a narrow region.
  • High Directionality: The beam travels in a single, straight direction with very little divergence.

Frequency Range

Lasers can emit radiation across various regions of the electromagnetic spectrum, including infrared (IR), visible, ultraviolet (UV), and even X-rays, depending on the active medium and laser design.

Essential Components of a Laser

  • Active Medium: The material in which laser action takes place (e.g., gas, crystal, semiconductor).
  • Pumping Mechanism: Supplies energy to the active medium to achieve population inversion.
  • Optical Resonator: Usually consists of two mirrors that reflect light back and forth through the medium to amplify the laser beam.
  • Power Supply: Provides the necessary electrical or optical energy to drive the laser.

The uniqueness of lasers lies in their ability to produce coherent light, which is essential for applications in communications, surgery, engineering, and science.

Historical Note

The initial invention of the laser opened up a new era in optics and physics. Lasers are now ubiquitous in technologies such as barcode scanners, fiber-optic communications, laser surgery, and scientific research. The ruby laser demonstrated by Theodore Maiman operated in pulsed mode and emitted deep red light.
