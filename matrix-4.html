Double Integrals

Double integrals allow integration over two-variable functions, often representing volume under surfaces.

Notation: ∬R f(x, y) dA = ∬R f(x, y) dxdy

These integrals are crucial for computing surface areas, mass distributions, and average values over regions in the plane.

Tip: Always examine the integration region (R) carefully; sketching helps visualize bounds.

Change of Order of Integration

Switch the order from dydx to dxdy (or vice versa) by rewriting integration bounds based on region sketch.

This technique is often necessary to simplify evaluation or when integrating over complex regions where one order is intractable.

Example:

From y: x² to 2x, x: 0 to 2 → y: 0 to 4, x: y/2 to √y

Hint: When changing order, draw a bounding diagram to track curves intersecting the region.

Change of Variables (Cartesian to Polar)

Use x = r cos(θ), y = r sin(θ) and convert dA to r dr dθ when changing to polar coordinates.

Polar coordinates simplify integrals over circular or radial regions, making integration faster and cleaner.

Jacobian: r, because of transformation stretching in area elements.

Area Enclosed by Plane Curves

Find area between curves using definite integrals: A = ∫ab [f(x) - g(x)] dx

Example:

Between y = √x and y = x² from x = 0 to 1 → A = ∫(√x - x²) dx = 1/3

Insight: Always identify upper and lower curves, or right and left, depending on x or y integration.

Triple Integrals

Triple integrals extend integration to three dimensions for volume, mass, or other 3D properties.

These are essential in fields like physics and engineering where three-dimensional analysis is required.

Order of integration can be rearranged (dxdydz, dydzdx, etc.) based on region simplicity and function form.

Example: Volume of a box: ∫02∫03∫01 dzdydx = 6
